Diabetes and Hypertension

Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic condition affecting how the body processes blood sugar (glucose). Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the pancreas produces little or no insulin. Type 2 diabetes is the most common form where the body does not use insulin effectively.

Symptoms of diabetes include increased thirst, frequent urination, extreme hunger, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, slow-healing sores, and frequent infections. Type 2 diabetes symptoms develop slowly and may be absent for years.

Diabetes management includes blood sugar monitoring, healthy diet, regular exercise, oral medications, and insulin therapy. Complications include heart disease, nerve damage, kidney damage, eye damage, and foot problems.

Hypertension (High Blood Pressure) is a condition where blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated. Normal blood pressure is below 120/80 mmHg. Hypertension is defined as consistently 130/80 mmHg or higher.

Hypertension is called the silent killer because it often has no symptoms. When symptoms occur they may include headache, dizziness, shortness of breath, nosebleeds, and flushing. These symptoms usually only occur in hypertensive crisis (above 180/120 mmHg).

Management of hypertension includes lifestyle changes (reduced salt intake, exercise, weight loss, limiting alcohol), and medications including ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics. Uncontrolled hypertension leads to heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and vision loss.
