Asthma and Bronchitis

Asthma is a chronic lung condition that causes inflammation and narrowing of the airways, making breathing difficult. Airways become swollen, and the muscles around them tighten when asthma is triggered.

Asthma symptoms include wheezing (a whistling sound when breathing), shortness of breath, chest tightness, coughing especially at night or early morning. Triggers include allergens (pollen, dust mites, pet dander), air pollution, exercise, cold air, respiratory infections, and stress.

Asthma is diagnosed with spirometry (lung function test), peak flow measurement, and allergy testing. Treatment includes quick-relief inhalers (bronchodilators like albuterol) for immediate symptoms and long-term control medications (inhaled corticosteroids) to reduce inflammation.

Bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchial tubes (airways that carry air to and from the lungs). Acute bronchitis is usually caused by viruses and resolves within 1-3 weeks. Chronic bronchitis is a type of COPD with persistent cough producing mucus for at least 3 months per year for 2 consecutive years.

Symptoms of bronchitis include cough with mucus (may be clear, yellow, or green), shortness of breath, wheezing, chest discomfort, fever, chills, and fatigue. Acute bronchitis is usually viral and antibiotics are not effective. Treatment includes rest, fluids, cough suppressants, and bronchodilators if wheezing is present.

Chronic bronchitis is associated with smoking and is treated with bronchodilators, steroids, pulmonary rehabilitation, and oxygen therapy in severe cases. Smoking cessation is the most important treatment.
