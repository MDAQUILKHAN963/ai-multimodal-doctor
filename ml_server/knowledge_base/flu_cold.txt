Influenza (Flu) and Common Cold

Influenza (Flu) is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It can cause mild to severe illness and can lead to hospitalization or death in vulnerable populations.

Flu symptoms come on suddenly and include fever or chills, cough, sore throat, runny or stuffy nose, muscle or body aches, headaches, fatigue, and vomiting and diarrhea (more common in children). Fever may not always be present.

The common cold is also a viral infection of the upper respiratory tract. Colds are usually milder than flu. Symptoms include runny nose, congestion, sore throat, cough, slight body aches, mild headache, sneezing, and low-grade fever. Symptoms appear 1-3 days after exposure.

Key differences between flu and cold: Flu has sudden onset, high fever, severe body aches, and significant fatigue. Cold has gradual onset, rarely causes fever, and is mainly nose and throat symptoms.

Treatment for flu includes rest, fluids, antiviral medications (oseltamivir/Tamiflu) within 48 hours of symptom onset for high-risk groups. For colds, treatment is symptomatic - rest, fluids, decongestants, and pain relievers.

Prevention includes annual flu vaccination, regular handwashing, avoiding close contact with sick people, covering mouth when coughing or sneezing, and not touching eyes, nose, or mouth with unwashed hands.
